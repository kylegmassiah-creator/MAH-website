Ancient texts.
Modern media.

The intersection.

Ancient texts don't stay in the past. Thucydides explains modern war on social media. Homer's Iliad shapes blockbuster films and video games. Spartan imagery appears at political rallies. MAH investigates how - and what it means when history is used, misused and weaponised in contemporary media.

Featured Research

Tweeting the War:
Herodotus, Thucydides
& Ukraine

An innovative interdisciplinary pilot study - Cardiff, Exeter & Oxford

Using applied linguistics and media discourse analysis, TtW examines how ancient Greek historians - Herodotus and Thucydides - are invoked across social media in the context of the Russia–Ukraine war. From the Melian Dialogue to Spartan symbolism to democratic ideals, the project traces how fifth-century BCE texts are mobilised, adapted and misused in the digital age.

What the Research Explores

Three core questions driving our analysis

The project brought together ancient historians, sociolinguists and digital discourse analysts to examine how - and why - modern audiences reach back 2,500 years to make sense of contemporary conflict.

Who is tweeting the ancient historians?

Analysing the demographics, affiliations and patterns of social media users who invoke Herodotus and Thucydides in the context of the Russia–Ukraine war - and what this tells us about modern classical reception.

How are the ancient texts being transformed?

Examining how meanings and forms of ancient historical texts are reconfigured in the digital age - which elements travel, which are stripped away, and which are deliberately distorted for political effect.

What does this mean for education?

How digital technologies can inspire new applications of ancient history in the classroom - from university-level seminars to secondary school workshops exploring fake news, historical truth and media literacy.
